<h1>Sky Constellation Shapes and Resources</h1>
<p class=quote>
null<br>
from an undefined<br>
place, <br>
undefined<br>
create (a place)<br>
an account<br>
of us
<br class=br1><span class=norm>&mdash; Viorica Hrincu</span>
</p>
<p>Having recently drawn a few skycharts (<a href="/universe-superclusters-and-voids">Superclusters & Voids</a>, <a href="http://www.sanctuaryproject.eu">Sanctuary Project</a>), I was frustrated by the lack of parsable resources for the <a href="https://www.iau.org/public/themes/constellations/">Constellations</a>. Not being able to find a plain-text parsable definition of the constellation figures proved impossible, I created my own.

<p>Quotes on this page are from my conversation with the folks at <a href="https://skyandtelescope.org">Sky & Telescope</a> and <a href="https://www.iau.org/public/themes/constellations/">IAU</a>.

<h2 id=who-is-in-charge-here->1 <b>&middot;</b> Who is in charge here?</h2>
</div>
<p>You might be surprised to learn (I certainly was) that while the <a href="https://www.iau.org">International Astronomial Union</a> has a <a href="https://www.iau.org/science/scientific_bodies/working_groups/280/">working group on stars names (WGSN)</a>, nobody is actually in charge of defining a canonical set of constellation shapes. I know &mdash; the horror, the horror.

<p class=quote>
While the IAU constellation boundaries, constellation names, and star
names have 'official' IAU values/names, the lines connecting the stars
are subject to the artist.

<br class=br1>&mdash;<a href="https://iau.org/administration/membership/individual/10983/">Eric Mamajek</a>
(chair, IAU WG Star Names)

</p>
<div class=tocsection>
<div class=top><a href="#top"><span class="material-icons-round">keyboard_arrow_up</span></a></div>
<h2 id=constellation-figures>2 <b>&middot;</b> Constellation figures</h2>
</div>
<p>While the IAU maintains a <a href="https://www.iau.org/public/themes/constellations/">page of constellation maps</a>, these maps were actually produced at <a href="https://skyandtelescope.org">Sky & Telescope magazine</a> by <a href="https://skyandtelescope.org/about-us/roger-w-sinnott/">Roger Sinnott</a>, who put out the excellent <a href="https://shopatsky.com/products/jumbo-pocket-sky-atlas-second-edition/">Pocket Sky Atlas</a>. 

<p class=quote>
&ldquo;The constellation figures on the IAU set of charts came from
Sky & Telescope, where they were designed by Alan MacRobert and have
been used in the magazine since the early 1990s. They aren't official
in any sense, but Alan did give them a lot of careful thought.&rdquo;
<br class=br1><span class=norm>&mdash; <a href="https://skyandtelescope.org/about-us/roger-w-sinnott/">Roger Sinnott</a> (Sky & Telescope)</span>
</p>
<p>The constellation shapes themselves were designed for Sky & Telescope by <a href="https://skyandtelescope.org/about-us/alan-m-macrobert">Alan MacRobert</a>. Alan was influenced by shapes drawn by H.A. Rey in his book <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Stars:_A_New_Way_to_See_Them">Stars: A New Way to See Them</a> but in many cases adjusted them to preserve earlier traditions.


<p class=quote>
&ldquo;I'm gratified at how the astronomical world has been
gravitating to our constellation figures over the years, starting
with the IAU. I put a great deal of thought into them.&rdquo;

<br class=br1><span class=norm>&mdash; <a href="https://skyandtelescope.org/about-us/alan-m-macrobert/">Alan MacRobert</a> (Sky & Telescope)</span>
</p>
<div class=tocsection>
<div class=top><a href="#top"><span class="material-icons-round">keyboard_arrow_up</span></a></div>
<h3 id=figures-can-connect>2.1 <b>&middot;</b> Figures can connect</h3>
</div>
<p>A figure for a constellation may connect to one or more
neighbouring figures. For example, the figure of Carina connects to
Puppis and Vela.




<div class="image" style="width:620px;"><div>
<img class="zoom" data-original="img/carina-constellation.png"  src="img/carina-constellation.png"  alt="Martin Krzywinski @MKrzywinski mkweb.bcgsc.ca" width="620px">
<div class=caption>
<span class=vsmall>&#9650;</span> <b>CARINA CONSTELLATION | </b>Carina connects to Vela (via Regor and Alsephina), Puppis (via Canopus) and Pyxis.


</div>
</div></div>
<div class=tocsection>
<div class=top><a href="#top"><span class="material-icons-round">keyboard_arrow_up</span></a></div>
<h3 id=connections-may-have-gaps>2.2 <b>&middot;</b> Connections may have gaps</h3>
</div>
<p>Some connections terminate short of their endpoint &mdash; these gaps
were intentionally included by Alan in his original designs. The gaps
are indicated by a flag in the figure records and a unit vector along
the line is included on either end to help you draw the line short of
its end.

<p>In the case of Carina (above), you can see that its connections to
the stars of Vela and Puppis have a gap.

<p>Two other examples (of many) are Hydra and Orion.

<div class=top><a href="#top"><span class="material-icons-round">keyboard_arrow_up</span></a></div>
<h4 id=hydra>2.2.1 <b>&middot;</b> Hydra</h4>
</div>
<div class="vmar4" style="width:620px;"><div>
<img class="zoom" data-original="img/hydra-constellation.png"  src="img/hydra-constellation.png"  alt="Martin Krzywinski @MKrzywinski mkweb.bcgsc.ca" width="620px">
<div class=caption>
<span class=vsmall>&#9650;</span> <b>HYDRA CONSTELLATION | </b>In the long serpentine figure of Hydra, there are short gaps around Alpha and Beta Crateris.


</div>
</div></div>
<p class=quote>

&ldquo;There are other cases, too, when our lines do not quite connect to
stars. For example, in the long serpentine figure of Hydra, we
intentionally left short gaps around &alpha; and &beta; Crateris. That's
because those stars officially belong to Crater, rather than Hydra,
even though they take part in the geometrical figures of both.&rdquo;

<br class=br1><span class=norm>&mdash; <a href="https://skyandtelescope.org/about-us/roger-w-sinnott/">Roger Sinnott</a> (Sky &
Telescope)</span>
</p>

<div class=top><a href="#top"><span class="material-icons-round">keyboard_arrow_up</span></a></div>
<h4 id=orion>2.2.2 <b>&middot;</b> Orion</h4>
</div>
<div class="vmar4" style="width:620px;"><div>
<img class="zoom" data-original="img/orion-constellation.png"  src="img/orion-constellation.png"  alt="Martin Krzywinski @MKrzywinski mkweb.bcgsc.ca" width="620px">
<div class=caption>
<span class=vsmall>&#9650;</span> <b>ORION CONSTELLATION | </b>Orion has a raised arm (the single line from <i>&alpha;</i> Orionis to <i>&mu;</i> and the <i>&xi;&ndash;&nu;</i> pair) wielding a heavy club (outlined by <i>&xi;&ndash;&nu;&ndash;&chi;</i><sup>1</sup><i>&ndash;&chi;</i><sup>2</sup>).


</div>
</div></div>
<p class=quote>

&ldquo;Alan [added gaps] quite consciously, to give the simple
geometric figure a shape more like a man with two legs (i.e., like
Orion, the Hunter, in mythology). And as a hunter, Orion has a raised
arm (the single line from <i>&alpha;</i> Orionis to <i>&mu;</i> and
the <i>&xi;&ndash;&nu;</i> pair) wielding a heavy club (outlined
by <i>&xi;&ndash;&nu;&ndash;&chi;</i><sup>1</sup><i>&ndash;&chi;</i><sup>2</sup>).

<br class=br1>

Among the charts there are several other cases of a line going to the
rough midpoint of two close stars, because the observer thinks of them
as a pair rather than two distinct stars.&rdquo;

<br class=br1><span class=norm>&mdash; [Roger
Sinnott](https://skyandtelescope.org/about-us/roger-w-sinnott/) (Sky &
Telescope)</span>
<p>
<div class=tocsection>
<div class=top><a href="#top"><span class="material-icons-round">keyboard_arrow_up</span></a></div>
<h3 id=carina-intersects-volans>2.3 <b>&middot;</b> Carina intersects Volans</h3>
</div>
<p>Connections of figures do not intersect except for the edge case of
Carina and Volans. The <i>&alpha;&ndash;&beta;</i> connection of
Carina crosses Volans and intersects its <i>&alpha;&ndash;&beta;</i>
and <i>&alpha;&ndash;&epsilon;</i> connections.

<p>This resolved by introducing a gap in the Carina <i>&alpha;&ndash;&beta;</i> line. 




<div class="vmar4" style="width:620px;"><div>
<img class="zoom" data-original="img/volans-constellation.png"  src="img/volans-constellation.png"  alt="Martin Krzywinski @MKrzywinski mkweb.bcgsc.ca" width="620px">
<div class=caption>
<span class=vsmall>&#9650;</span> <b>VOLANS CONSTELLATION | </b>A line in Carina cuts through Volans.


</div>
</div></div>
<p class=quote>

&ldquo;I forgot to mention one more situation where a gap occurs in
the [figure] of a constellation, at least as implemented in our
S&T software. We did not want a line belonging to one constellation
to cross or intersect a line of another constellation. The best
example (maybe the only example) appears on the attached chart of
Volans.&rdquo;

<br class=br1><span class=norm>&mdash; <a href="https://skyandtelescope.org/about-us/roger-w-sinnott/">Roger Sinnott</a> (Sky & Telescope)</span>
</p>
<p>The <i>&alpha;&ndash;&beta;</i> gap is not encoded in my file
because its position depends on the projection.

<p class=quote>
&ldquo;In our software, we took care of this and other gaps in lines
by adding fictitious stars to the database for the lines to end
on. These fictitious stars lie on the great circle between the two
real stars that define the line. This way, a graphic artist who
finalizes a chart does not have to remember to insert needed gaps
manually.&rdquo;

<br class=br1><span class=norm>&mdash; <a href="https://skyandtelescope.org/about-us/roger-w-sinnott/">Roger Sinnott</a> (Sky & Telescope)</span>
</p>
<div class=tocsection>
<div class=top><a href="#top"><span class="material-icons-round">keyboard_arrow_up</span></a></div>
<h2 id=conversation-with-alan-macrobert>3 <b>&middot;</b> Conversation with Alan MacRobert</h2>
</div>
<p>I had a great exchange with Alan about the figures. 

<p>Here, he comments about his design philosophy.

<p class=quote>
Each [figure] is a compromise between competing priorities.
<br class=br1>
The lines must include the lines that the eye can't help but naturally
see. Two bright stars near each other must be connected because
that's what the eye does, no escaping it.
<br class=br1>
Figures should look like the thing named if reasonably possible. I
started with H. A. Rey's groundbreaking figures in his constellation
guide <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Stars:_A_New_Way_to_See_Them">The Stars, a New Way to See Them</a> (1952). 
<br class=br1>
But Rey committed four sins.
<br class=br1>
<span style="display:block;margin-left:1em;">1 &mdash; He occasionally fudged star positions
slightly to make his figures work better.</span>
<br class=br1>
<span style="display:block;margin-left:1em;">2 &mdash; He sometimes used stars too
faint for most modern skywatchers to see.</span> 
<br class=br1>
<span style="display:block;margin-left:1em;">3  &mdash; He often connected stars
with line patterns too complicated for the eye to see easily, while
skipping the lines between brighter stars that the eye can't help but
see.</span>
<br class=br1>
<span style="display:block;margin-left:1em;">4 &mdash; And, he paid no attention to the constellations' deep heritage in how they have been oriented and depicted since Greco-Roman times. This
often makes them conflict with the representations that were the basis
of it all and that are seen throught astronomy literature.</span>
<br class=br1>
I tried to make realistic stick figures that <b>did</b> match ancient
representations, as recorded in the Almagest star names ('foot,'
'knee', 'tail', etc.) and old depictions going back to the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Farnese_Atlas">Farnese Globe</a>.
<br class=br1><span class=norm>&mdash; <a href="https://skyandtelescope.org/about-us/alan-m-macrobert/">Alan MacRobert</a> (Sky & Telescope)</span>
</p>
<div class="image" style="margin-left:1em;;width:300px;;float:right;"><div>
<img class="zoom" data-original="img/virgo-constellation.png"  src="img/virgo-constellation.png"  alt="Martin Krzywinski @MKrzywinski mkweb.bcgsc.ca" width="300px">
<div class=caption>
<span class=vsmall>&#9650;</span> <b>VIRGO CONSTELLATION | </b>Virgo is holding the ear of grain (Spica) in one hand and sowing seeds on the springtime fields with her other hand.


</div>
</div></div>
<div class=tocsection>
<div class=top><a href="#top"><span class="material-icons-round">keyboard_arrow_up</span></a></div>
<h3 id=virgo>3.1 <b>&middot;</b> Virgo</h3>
</div>
<p class=quote>
The Virgo I eventually came up with, for instance, not only
matches her ancient arrangement but for the first time, as far as I
know, matches her activity: holding the ear of grain Spica in one hand
and sowing seeds on the springtime fields with her other hand.
<br class=br1>
I suspect that I rediscovered the original Virgo as seen by farmers
and herdsmen outdoors at night before the 'fine arts' took over
(starting with the artist hired to paint the constellations on a
ceiling in the palace of Philip of Macedon. A king doesn't want grubby
shepherds' stick figures, he wants Fine Art to show off. And so it
began.)

<br class=br1><span class=norm>&mdash; <a href="https://skyandtelescope.org/about-us/alan-m-macrobert/">Alan MacRobert</a> (Sky & Telescope)</span>
</p>

<div class=caption>
<span class=vsmall>&#9650;</span> <b>HERCULES CONSTELLATION | </b>The head, the knee and the club.


</div>
</div></div>

<h3 id=hercules>3.2 <b>&middot;</b> Hercules</h3>
</div>
<p class=quote>
Similarly, I went with the Hercules who has 'Head of the Giant' as
his head star, 'Knee' as his knee star, and 'Club' as the top of his
club. 
<br class=br1>This makes Hercules hold out his lion skin in front of him,
echoing Orion also waving a club over his head and holding out a lion
skin in front of him. I doubt that was a coincidence.
<br class=br1><span class=norm>&mdash; <a href="https://skyandtelescope.org/about-us/alan-m-macrobert/">Alan MacRobert</a> (Sky & Telescope)</span>

<h1><a href="

https://www.annualreviews.org/content/journals/10.1146/annurev-statistics-112723-034555
">Crafting 10 Years of Statistics Explanations: Points of Significance</a></h1>
<div class="sans timestamp vmar1">
Sun 09-03-2025
</div>
<p><i>I don’t have good luck in the match points. &mdash;Rafael Nadal, Spanish tennis player</i></p>
<p>Points of Significance is an ongoing series of short articles about statistics in Nature Methods that started in 2013. Its aim is to provide clear explanations of essential concepts in statistics for a nonspecialist audience. The articles favor heuristic explanations and make extensive use of simulated examples and graphical explanations, while maintaining mathematical rigor. 

<p>Topics range from basic, but often misunderstood, such as uncertainty and <i>P</i>-values, to relatively advanced, but often neglected, such as the error-in-variables problem and the curse of dimensionality. More recent articles have focused on timely topics such as modeling of epidemics, machine learning, and neural networks. 

<p>In this article, we discuss the evolution of topics and details behind some of the story arcs, our approach to crafting statistical explanations and narratives, and our use of figures and numerical simulations as props for building understanding.

<h1><a href="

">Propensity score matching</a></h1>
<div class="sans timestamp vmar1">
Mon 16-09-2024
</div>
<p><i>I don’t have good luck in the match points. &mdash;Rafael Nadal, Spanish tennis player</i></p>
<p>In many experimental designs, we need to keep in mind the possibility of confounding variables, which may give rise to bias in the estimate of the treatment effect.




<div class="image" style="width:325px;"><div>
<a href="http://www.nature.com/articles/s41592-024-02405-4">
<img    src="/pointsofsignificance/img/pointsofsignificance-propensity-score-matching.png"  alt="Martin Krzywinski @MKrzywinski mkweb.bcgsc.ca" width="325px">
</a>
<div class=caption>
<span class=vsmall>&#9650;</span> Nature Methods Points of Significance column: Propensity score matching.


(<a href="http://www.nature.com/articles/s41592-024-02405-4">read</a>)
</div>
</div></div>
<p>If the control and experimental groups aren't matched (or, roughly, similar enough), this bias can arise. 

<p>Sometimes this can be dealt with by randomizing, which on average can balance this effect out. When randomization is not possible, propensity score matching is an excellent strategy to match control and experimental groups.

<h1><a href="

https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/00236772241247106
">Understanding p-values and significance</a></h1>
<div class="sans timestamp vmar1">
Tue 24-09-2024
</div>
<p><i>P</i>-values combined with estimates of effect size are used to assess the importance of experimental results. However, their interpretation can be invalidated by selection bias when testing multiple hypotheses, fitting multiple models or even informally selecting results that seem interesting after observing the data. 

<p>We offer an introduction to principled uses of <i>p</i>-values (targeted at the non-specialist) and identify questionable practices to be avoided.
